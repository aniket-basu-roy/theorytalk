This is a weekly series of informal talks given by people enthusiastic about Theoretical Computer Science. The goal is to have an interactive session discussing papers, book chapters or anything worthwhile that would broaden our common knowledge. The speaker should consider the background of the audience and spend ample time in laying the basics of the material before going on to the nitty-gritties. Also note this is not a platform to prove one's smartness but mutually learn something new.



April 27, 2015

Sparse Polynomial Identity Testing
Vineet Nair

Consider the problem of Polynomial Identity Testing(PIT): we are given an arithmetic circuit computing an n variate, degree d polynomial over some field and we have to determine whether that polynomial is identically zero or not. In this presentation we look at blackbox PIT, where we can just query the circuit on different points from the underlying field, Klivans and Spielman showed that this can be done in poly(n,d,m) time, where m is the number of monomials in the given polynomial. Hence if m and d is polynomial in n, we can do blackbox PIT in polynomial time. The paper by Klivans and Spielman is here.

April 15, 2015

Reed-Solomon codes - Why and How ?
Speaker: Ninad Rajgopal

  • Coding Theory 101 - A good reference for the basics of coding theory which I covered are the course handouts by Venkatesan Guruswami's course at CMU in Spring, 2010.
  • Reed-Solomon (RS) Codes - Most of my presentation followed notes6 (Properties) and notes7 (Decoding), of the course mentioned above.   • $O(n^3)$ independent result using lyapunov function for 1-dimension [link].
  • $O(n^4)$ in any dimension (independent of dimension) [link].

  • A lower bound of $O(n^2)$ in 1-dimension(Dumbbell configuration) [link].

  • Our results on social HK model and non-deterministic HK model - [link].
  • There is a recent result on the convergence for a special case of the heterogenous version of the problem. Here, the agents have a confidence bound of either 0 or 1. For more details, you can go through this paper.
  • These are the two remarkable papers from Bernard Chazelle in this area, where he proves convergence and periodicity and other crazy results for general graphs. See here and here for these results.

March 23, 2015

The Lovasz Local Lemma
 Speaker: Ninad Rajgopal

  • The best reference for the probabilistic method - The Probabilistic Method (Alon and Spencer)
    • Chapter 5 talks about Lovasz Local Lemma and it's applications.
  • Another nice reference is this set of lecture notes by Matousek and Vondrak. The Probabilistic Method (Matousek and Vondrak)
  • I could not cover the algorithmic aspects of the LLL. There have been many papers which have looked at the constructive (algorithmic) versions of this technique, with the most famous one being the paper by Moser and Tardos, which gives a constructive proof for the LLL. Hopefully, I might be able to bring it up on a later date.
